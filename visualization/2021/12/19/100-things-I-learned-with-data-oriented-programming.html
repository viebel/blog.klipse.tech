A hundred things I learned writing my first technical book "Data-Oriented Programming"

December 19, 2021 dop theory

https://twitter.com/threadapalooza https://twitter.com/dan_abramov/status/1470613731071696896

  1. Writing a technical book is much harder than writing blog posts.
  2. Writing my first technical book without a publisher would have been a MISSION: IMPOSSIBLE!
  3. Front matter is the last part an author writes.
  4. Each piece of the book content must be clear and interesting. Each part, each chapter, each section, each paragraph, each sentence.
  5. A possible way to make things clear is go from concrete to abstract.
  6. A possible way to make things interesting is to teach the material as a story.
  7. The “why” is more important than the “what”.
  8. The “what” is more important than the “how”.
  9. An average writer makes the reader think the author is smart. A good writer makes the reader think the reader is smart.
  10. A technical book is written for MQRs (Minimal Qualified Readers). It allows the author to assume what knowledge the reader already have.
  11. When writing a story, things should be not be left abstract. For instance, instead of having a character drinking a coffee at a bar it’s better to have them drinking an espresso at the french coffee shop of the 42nd street.
  12. Checking book sales could be addictive.
  13. People on Reddit could feel hurt by opinions that take them out of their comfort zone.
  14. On Reddit, when people feel hurt, they could become violent.
  15. Making a good Table of Contents is crucial as it is the first part of the book potential readers will encounter.
  16. Making a good Table of Contents is hard as you need to figure out what you really want to talk about.
  17. Table of Contents might evolve as you write the book.
  18. Before writing a chapter, you should formulate to yourself what is the main objective of the chapter.
  19. If a chapter has two main objectives, it’s a sign that you should split it into two chapters.
  20. Before writing a chapter, you should write a chapter plan.
  21. A possible way to make things interesting is to use examples.
  22. A possible way to make things clear is to use examples with increasing level of difficulty.
  23. Do not hesitate to highlight sentences that convey an important message.
  24. When you start to write a technical book involves a descent amount of research even if you consider yourself as an expert in the field.
  25. Finding attractive but accurate titles to book chapters is an art.
  26. You can learn a lot from a failed attempt to write a book, provided that you put your ago aside.
  27. You cannot write a Wikipedia article about the topic of your book until it is mentioned by other sources.
  28. AsciiDoc rocks!
  29. PlantUML rocks!
  30. NeoVim rocks!
  31. It makes lot of sense to use a source control software for your manuscript.
  32. You need to be very careful not to hurt the sensitivity of any of your readers.
  33. A good technical reviewer is an asset.
  34. A good publisher is your partner.
  35. You could make more dollars per copy by self-publishing but you’d probably sell much less copies.
  36. Asking early feedback from external reviewers is a great idea!
  37. Releasing an early version of the book is super-useful.
  38. Finding a good book title is hard.
  39. Finding a good book subtitle is even harder.
  40. Having your book featured on HackerNews home page do not mean selling lots of copies.
  41. Twitter is a great medium to share ideas from a book.
  42. If you are lucky, writing a book could sometimes take you to flow.
  43. My real motivation for writing a book was neither to be famous nor to be rich. It only wanted to accomplish a child’s dream.
  44. It’s hard to find your writing style.
  45. Once you have found the your writing style, the writing flows.
  46. It’s OK to stop writing for a couple of weeks.
  47. Do not bury the lead.
  48. Be open to critics - even when they hurt your ego.
  49. The more you write, the more you like it.
  50. It’s safe to assume that every developer can read JavaScript.
  51. It’s a great feeling to mention the work of other authors.
  52. You should make sure that each and every code snippet - that appears in your book - runs as expected.
  53. Invoking “it’s so obvious I don’t need to explain it” is not an acceptable argument.
  54. Writing your teaching materials as a dialogue between an expert and a novice is a very useful process in order to figure out what questions your materials might raise in your reader’s mind.
  55. Rewriting a chapter from scratch because you forgot to save your work could be a blessing as it might lead to a material of higher quality.
  56. Writing in a coffee shop makes me feel as an author from a movie, but in fact I am more productive at home.
  57. Writing a preface - after the whole manuscript is ready - is really fun!
  58. Include at least 2 or 3 diagrams in every chapter. It makes the material more fun to read and easier to grasp.
  59. Draw your diagrams on a sheet of paper before using a drawing software.
  60. When a section is more difficult to read that the others, let your readers know about it.
  61. Asking a friend or a colleague to read your work in progress is not a productive idea.
  62. When you are blocked in the middle of a chapter, it might be a sign that you need to rethink the chapter.
  63. Adapting parts of your book to blog posts could be a good idea. But you need to resist the temptation of copy-pasting verbatim.
  64. If feels great when someone with 50K followers tweets about the fun he had reading your book.
  65. Writing an appendix is much easier than writing a chapter.
  66. Throwing away some (good) ideas is sometimes necessary. Not easy but necessary.
  67. Using humour in a technical book is possible. Hopefully, it’s appreciated.
  68. You should write the chapter introduction after all the other parts of the chapter are written.
  70. Writing a hundred things you learned from writing a book is not as difficult as it may seem.
